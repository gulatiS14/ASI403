1. Life Science Sub-system

1.1 Context of Problem

Professional Mars Rovers, including NASA’s Curiosity and Perseverance, deploy powerful but resource-intensive instruments such as X-ray diffraction, laser-induced breakdown spectroscopy and mass spectrometry to identify geochemical and organic markers. Although highly sophisticated, these devices possess a few downsides including:

  • High Power and Mass: Spaceflight-grade instruments are large and energy-hungry.
  • Ambiguous Biotic vs. Abiotic Signals: Detecting carbon-based molecules is not always enough to confirm biological origins.

On the other hand, student-Led Mars Rovers often adopt simpler and lower-cost methods such as colorimetric or small-scale optical detection, typically for for proteins or pigments. While more feasible within typical university budgets, these approaches:

  • Provide narrow or shallow detection scope
  • Risk false negatives or false positive as determining the results using naked eyes is relatively unreliable
  • May struggle in dusty conditions, as soil coloration can confound results

Hence, two principal value propositions for the life science subsystem were determined in the previous semester as follows:

  • A comprehensive science plan that ensures high-accuracy detection of potential biosignatures which provides critical insights into the habitability of selected sites that replicate Martian conditions.
  • A science plan that enables real-time, in-situ onboard analysis of soil samples for potential life signals with minimal reliance on external technical support which enhances the rover's scientific capability and overall mission value.

Building on these value propositions, the following design statements guide the development of a solution that can:

Design statement (1): Detect multiple type of biosignatures

Design statement (2): Enable real-time detection of potential life markers onboard the Mars rover

Design statement (3): Provide results interpretable by minimal amount of onboard device with a short duration

This semester, I continue to develop solutions that closely adhere to the established criteria. However, several iterations have been made to account for the engineering complexities involved in constructing the onboard system. In addition, given the high cost and limited availability of the necessary chemicals in the NUS laboratory, my focus in this semester is on refining the chemistry experiments through literature review and only starting to commence hands-on testing in the laboratory once the experimental flow is approved by the engineering team.

1.2.2 Concept 2: Pivot to Spectrofluorometry

With the UREX team’s timely delivery of a compact and low-power spectrofluorometer, we reverted to our primary plan:

  • Integrate the spectrofluorometer into a sealed test chamber for in-situ soil analysis.
  • Use fluorescent markers and dyes that respond to various biosignatures.

This pivot drastically increased detection power and consolidated the subsystem design, which allows us to retire colorimetric assays before further testing and iterations.

For the spectrofluorometric tests, we initially mapped out five key biosignatures for the spectrofluorometer to detect:

  • Proteins: Smaller amino acid fragments survive Martian radiation better but intact proteins reduce false positives by confirming more specific biological origins.
  • Nucleic Acids: Strongly life-specific molecules whose partial fragments remain key indicators of past or present biological processes.
  • FAD (Flavin Adenine Dinucleotide): Signals active or historic cellular respiration and therefore highlights underlying metabolic activity.
  • FDA (Fluorescein Diacetate): Reveals live enzyme activity through fluorescence, which offers a sensitive measure of current microbial viability.
  • Enantiomeric Excess: Confirms biotic origins by detecting an imbalance in amino acid chirality that is nearly impossible to replicate through abiotic processes.

This comprehensive suite addresses major structural, metabolic and functional markers of life which all measurable by suitable excitation-emission scanning in one spectrofluorometer.

However, due to time constraints and the complexities of adding specialized reagents for enantiomeric excess detection, we opted to omit this test. While analyzing amino acid chirality could help further minimize false positives, we prioritized URC 2025 eligibility by focusing on our remaining four methods, which already offer a robust and comprehensive approach to life detection.

1.4 Fulfilment of deliverables

The proposed experiment successfully addresses all the outlined design statements through a compact and efficient life detection strategy. First, it fulfills Design Statement 1 by targeting a diverse range of biosignatures—specifically amino acids, DNA/RNA, flavin adenine dinucleotide (FAD), and fluorescein diacetate (FDA). These markers were selected based on their biological relevance and detectability under Martian conditions, thus enabling comprehensive screening for multiple types of potential life-related molecules.

In alignment with Design Statement 2, the experiment is designed to support real-time detection onboard the Mars rover. The use of fluorescence-based detection allows for rapid spectral acquisition, with each measurement requiring less than one minute. This fast response time enables efficient data collection during field operations, minimizing delays and maximizing mission productivity.

Lastly, the setup satisfies Design Statement 3 by being both equipment-light and time-efficient. The entire workflow requires only a single spectrofluorometer, significantly reducing the demand for bulky instrumentation and power consumption onboard the rover. Additionally, the complete detection process—from sample preparation to fluorescence reading—can be completed in under 20 minutes, ensuring timely and interpretable results without overburdening the rover’s processing capabilities.

1.5 Shortcomings and Future Work

One key limitation observed in the current experimental setup is the fluorescence spectral overlap between DNA/RNA and the buffer solution. The characteristic peaks of DNA/RNA are found to be too close to those of the buffer, which increases the risk of false positives and may compromise the reliability and accuracy of result interpretation. To mitigate this issue, extensive repeat testing is required to validate the consistency of the fluorescence signals obtained. Future work could involve exploring alternative detection strategies or modifying the buffer formulation to enhance peak distinction and reduce background interference.

Another potential shortcoming arises from the complex composition of Martian soil or regolith. The presence of minerals or perchlorates in the soil may either exhibit autofluorescence or act as quenching agents, leading to false positive signals or suppression of true fluorescence responses. This introduces additional challenges to signal clarity and biosignature identification. As part of future efforts, it is essential to expand the range of potential interference testing to include soil compositions that closely mimic Martian conditions. Investigating the interaction of the proposed experimental approach with known Martian analog materials can help refine detection reliability and support the development of more robust signal validation protocols.

1.4 Shortcomings Identified

The experiments conducted using the Ninhydrin, Biuret, and Xanthoproteic Tests demonstrated that protein detection in the presence of soil is feasible, though its effectiveness varies due to factors such as mixing and colour interference from soil particles. To address these limitations, the inclusion of stirring and filtration steps is considered. The experimental results showed that stirring facilitated the even distribution of proteins within the solution, ensuring consistent interaction with the reagents. Additionally, filtration are considered because it is known to be beneficial in creating a clearer solution by removing particulate matter that could obscure the results, thereby enhancing the accuracy and ease of observing colour changes (Jacobsen, 2004). Therefore, it shows a necessity for the mechanical subsystem, overseen by Jian Ling, to develop an effective stirring and filtration mechanism.
