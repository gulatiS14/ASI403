1.1 Context of Problem

This section aims to provide a comprehensive overview of current methodologies utilized by Mars rovers operating on Mars, as well as common life detection methods employed by other teams in the University Rover Challenge. By examining these methods, we seek to identify research gaps at both levels- professional Mars rovers and student-led rovers, and refine our problem statement to determine which gaps to address.

Table 1.1: Life Detection Methods and General Limitations of Professional and Student-led Mars Rovers

As seen in Table 1.1 above, both professional and student-led Mars rovers face notable limitations that highlight critical gaps in current life detection methods. These rovers are generally limited to surface-level analysis, which means they may miss potential subsurface biosignatures that could indicate past or present life. Their sensitivity is often restricted, making it difficult to detect low concentrations of proteins or organic compounds and leading to possible false negatives. Additionally, differentiating biological and non-biological sources with the current detection method is challenging, resulting in false positives and complicating data interpretation. Furthermore, current methods tend to focus on detecting specific biosignatures, which may lead to potentially overlooking other important markers, such as nucleic acids or the biosignature with different forms on Mars. This narrow focus leads to incomplete assessments of life signals and highlights the need for complementary techniques for more thorough analysis. Moreover, the instruments used are often bulky and consume substantial energy, limiting their portability and operational efficiency.

Value proposition and Design statement

To design a more effective onboard life detection system for Mars rovers, the following gaps must be addressed:

  • Broader Biosignature Detection: Implement a comprehensive science plan that enables the analysis of a diverse range of biomarkers to achieve a more holistic approach to life detection.
  • Enhanced Sensitivity: Develop advanced methods capable of detecting low concentrations of organic compounds to reduce the likelihood of false negatives.
  • Improved Biotic vs. Abiotic Differentiation: Establish reliable techniques for distinguishing between biological and non-biological sources of detected compounds.
  • Compact, Energy-Efficient Design: Create a science plan that relies on minimal yet efficient technical instrumentation to enhance portability and operational sustainability.

Addressing these gaps will allow us to develop a Mars rover that is capable of more comprehensive and reliable life detection, thereby enhancing the overall success of our rover in the URC Science Mission.

Considering the URC 2025 Science Mission requirements and the current gaps in life detection capabilities, two primary value propositions for life science subsystem have been identified:

  1. A comprehensive science plan that ensures high-accuracy detection of potential biosignatures which provides critical insights into the habitability of selected sites that replicate Martian conditions.
  2. A science plan that enables real-time, in-situ onboard analysis of soil samples for potential life signals with minimal reliance on external technical support which enhances the rover's scientific capability and overall mission value.

From these value propositions, the following design statements can be obtained to design a solution that can:

  • Design statement (1): Detect multiple type of biosignatures

  • Design statement (2): Enable real-time detection of potential life markers onboard the Mars rover

  • Design statement (3): Provide results interpretable by minimal amount of onboard device with a short duration

    Before delving into the concept development for the science plan, it is crucial to first define what constitutes life. This will provide a clearer framework for understanding and refining the focus of the experiments.

    The classification of living organisms is traditionally divided into five primary kingdoms: Animalia, Plantae, Fungi, Protista, and Monera (Whittaker, 1969). Table 1.2 provides an overview of these kingdoms, highlighting their respiration types, the air they interact with during metabolic processes and their fundamental building blocks.

    Based on Table 1.2 shown above, the essential building blocks of living organisms on Earth can be categorized into amino acids (proteins), nucleic acids (DNA/RNA), lipid acids (lipids) and carbohydrates.

    1.2.1 Concept 1

    Selecting which molecules to target for detection involves evaluating various biomolecules against criteria such as their specificity to life, stability in Martian conditions and feasibility of detection. These factors are vital for ensuring reliable and practical life detection on Mars, given the planet's harsh environment and limited detection opportunities. A weighted selection matrix as shown in Table 1.3 is used to assess each type of molecule against these criteria, with scores ranging from 0 to 5, where 0 indicates the lowest relevance to each criterion and 5 represents the highest.

    • Specificity to Life: Higher scores indicate better specificity to biological origins.
    • Stability in Martian Conditions: Higher scores suggest greater resilience to Mars's harsh conditions.
    • Detection Feasibility: Higher scores reflect the higher practicality of detecting each molecule type using available technology or chemical analysis methods.

    According to Table 1.3, the weighted concept selection matrix for identifying biosignatures indicative of life on Mars, amino acids (proteins) and nucleic acids (DNA/RNA) have emerged as the highest-scoring candidates and were therefore selected for life signal detection on the Mars rover.

    To strikes a balance between detection feasibility and specificity to life, proteins and nucleic acids were ultimately selected as the specific targeted biosignatures due to the high levels of radiation on Mars, which significantly increase the likelihood of molecular decomposition (Dartnell, 2011; Pavlov et al., 2012).

    As a result, smaller molecular fragments, such as nucleic acids and amino acids, have a higher probability of being detected compared to intact DNA/RNA and proteins. To prioritize specificity to life, nucleic acids were chosen for their enhanced detection potential on Mars. Proteins, while exhibiting greater stability under Martian conditions but lower specificity to life, were included to detect larger fragments and thereby improve the reliability of the results because larger fragments of biological building blocks have higher specificity to life and thus detecting larger fragments of biological building blocks reduces the likelihood of false positive results compared to detecting amino acids which have higher chance of being produced through abiotic processes (McKay et al., 1996).

    To address key gaps identified in current Mars rover life detection method in the “1.1: Context of Problem” section, FAD (Flavin Adenine Dinucleotide) and FDA (Fluorescein Diacetate) detection methods have been incorporated alongside nucleic acid and protein analysis to create a more effective and comprehensive science plan (Kieft & Phelps, 1997; Boulos et al., 1999).

    This strategy directly supports broader biosignature detection by enabling the identification of metabolic and enzymatic activities that extend beyond structural markers. FAD detection is crucial for signaling active or historic cellular respiration, which serves as evidence of energy production processes, while FDA acts as a marker of esterase enzyme activity, indicative of cell viability (Kieft & Phelps, 1997; Boulos et al., 1999). These additions enhance sensitivity by detecting low concentrations of active biomolecules, reducing the risk of false negatives.Moreover, this integrated approach addresses the need for improved biotic and abiotic differentiation by distinguishing between compounds of biological and non-biological origins.

    While nucleic acids and proteins can be remnants of past life or products of abiotic processes, FAD and FDA detection focus on current metabolic activity, making it easier to identify truly biotic signals. In addition, the enantiomeric excess testing is also considered to be included to further ensure reliability by identifying amino acid chirality linked to biological processes, confirming that detected biosignatures are of biotic origin, even when no current life signals are evident (Kvenvolden et al., 1970). The detection of enantiomeric excess of amino acids can be considered a biosignature because life on Earth predominantly uses specific chirality (L-amino acids) in its amino acids which results in an enantiomeric imbalance. This characteristic asymmetry is difficult to produce through abiotic processes, so finding a significant enantiomeric excess on Mars would strongly suggest that the amino acids detected are originated from biological activity rather than from non-living chemical reactions (Kvenvolden et al., 1970).

    After confirming the selected biosignatures for detection, the next step involves determining the appropriate detection method. A selection matrix shown in Table 1.4 was employed to evaluate the capability of each detection method for identifying the chosen biosignatures, where a score of 1 indicates the method can detect the biosignature and 0 indicates it cannot.

    As shown in Table 1.4, the spectrofluorometer was able to detect all five targeted biosignatures. Hence, the spectrofluorometer is selected as the primary instrument for our science module to conduct all experiments related to detecting the chosen biosignatures. This choice aligns with the goal of addressing the research gaps identified in the problem context, specifically the need for a compact and energy-efficient design for Mars missions. The spectrofluorometer's ability to perform multiple tests using a single, versatile instrument ensures minimal yet highly effective technical instrumentation, contributing to the sustainability and operational portability of the Mars rover's scientific payload.

    1.4 Shortcomings Identified

    The experiments conducted using the Ninhydrin, Biuret, and Xanthoproteic Tests demonstrated that protein detection in the presence of soil is feasible, though its effectiveness varies due to factors such as mixing and colour interference from soil particles. To address these limitations, the inclusion of stirring and filtration steps is considered. The experimental results showed that stirring facilitated the even distribution of proteins within the solution, ensuring consistent interaction with the reagents. Additionally, filtration are considered because it is known to be beneficial in creating a clearer solution by removing particulate matter that could obscure the results, thereby enhancing the accuracy and ease of observing colour changes (Jacobsen, 2004). Therefore, it shows a necessity for the mechanical subsystem, overseen by Jian Ling, to develop an effective stirring and filtration mechanism.
